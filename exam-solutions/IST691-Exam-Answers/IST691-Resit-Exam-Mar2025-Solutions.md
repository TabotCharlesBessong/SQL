## Question 1 (28 marks)

### a) On ethics (14 marks)

#### i) Why is ethics important in research? Give at least three examples. (4 marks)

- **Promotes truth and avoids error:** Norms against fabricating, falsifying, or misrepresenting data help research produce reliable knowledge.  
  *Example:* Fabricated data in a clinical trial can harm or kill patients.

- **Supports collaboration:** Standards on authorship, data sharing, and confidentiality build trust and accountability.  
  *Example:* Clear authorship and citation rules avoid disputes and encourage cooperation.

- **Builds public support:** When research is seen as honest and responsible, the public is more willing to fund and accept it.  
  *Example:* Ethical lapses (e.g. safety violations) can damage trust in an entire institution.

- **Protects participants and society:** Ethics ensures human and animal welfare, legal compliance, and social responsibility.  
  *Example:* Informed consent and IRB review protect participants from harm and exploitation.

---

#### ii) What is an ethical dilemma? What is the role of an IRB? (4 marks)

- **Ethical dilemma:** A situation where the researcher must weigh the **potential costs to participants** (e.g. stress, risk, deception) against the **potential benefits** of the research, and there is no clear, uncontroversial right answer.

- **Role of the IRB (Institutional Review Board):**  
  The IRB provides **independent review** of research involving human participants **before** the study begins. It **approves, exempts, or disapproves** proposals and can require changes. It protects participants’ **welfare, rights, and privacy** (e.g. informed consent, minimal risk) and monitors ongoing studies. Because judgments about costs and benefits are subjective, the decision should not rest with the researcher alone; the IRB ensures an external, ethical check.

---

#### iii) Issues addressed by: Honesty; Informed consent; Intellectual property; Human subjects protection (6 marks)

- **A. Honesty:**  
  Researchers must report data and results **truthfully** — no fabrication, falsification, or plagiarism. Honesty addresses **integrity in collecting, analyzing, and publishing** research so that findings are trustworthy and others can build on them.

- **B. Informed consent:**  
  Participants must **understand** the purpose, procedures, risks, and benefits of the study and **voluntarily agree** to take part. This addresses **autonomy and respect for persons**: no one should be enrolled without adequate information and free choice.

- **C. Intellectual property:**  
  Issues include **ownership of ideas, data, and outputs**; **proper attribution** (citation, authorship); **copyright and patents**; and **avoiding plagiarism**. Codes address how credit is given and how others’ work is used so that contributions are recognized and protected.

- **D. Human subjects protection:**  
  This addresses **minimizing harm** (physical, psychological, social, legal), **protecting vulnerable groups**, **privacy and confidentiality**, and **fair treatment** (e.g. equitable selection and benefits). Oversight (e.g. IRB) and consent are central to ensuring that research on humans is ethical.

### b) On hypothesis testing (6 marks)

#### i) Meaning and role of: Research question; Hypothesis (2 marks)

- **Research question:** The **specific question** (derived from the research problem) that asks what **relationship** exists between **two or more variables** and that can be answered by **collecting and analyzing data**. It **guides** the design, methods, and scope of the study.

- **Hypothesis:** A **testable prediction** about the relationship between variables (e.g. “IV will affect DV”). It gives a **tentative answer** to the research question, is grounded in theory or prior work, and **structures** the study and **statistical testing** (e.g. null vs alternative hypothesis).

---

#### ii) Empirical Rule (with reference to the normal curve) (2 marks)

For a **normal distribution**:

- About **68%** of the data lie within **1 standard deviation** of the mean (μ ± 1σ).  
- About **95%** lie within **2 standard deviations** (μ ± 2σ).  
- About **99.7%** lie within **3 standard deviations** (μ ± 3σ).

So most values are near the mean, and values beyond μ ± 3σ are rare.

---

#### iii) Role of the significance level (α) in hypothesis testing (2 marks)

**α** is the **maximum probability of a Type I error** we are willing to accept — i.e. **rejecting the null hypothesis when it is actually true**. It is set **before** the study (e.g. α = 0.05 or 0.10). It defines the **rejection region**: we reject the null if the p-value ≤ α. **Lower α** means we require **stronger evidence** before rejecting the null (fewer false positives but more risk of Type II error).

## Question 2 (22 marks)

### a) Meaning in the context of an experiment (6 marks)

- **(i) Extraneous variable:** Any variable **other than the independent variable** that can **influence the dependent variable**; if not controlled, it can threaten internal validity or act as a confound.

- **(ii) Internal validity:** The extent to which we can conclude that the **independent variable caused** the observed change in the dependent variable, rather than the change being due to extraneous or confounding variables.

- **(iii) External validity:** The extent to which the results of the experiment can be **generalized** to other **participants, settings, times, or measures** (i.e. beyond the specific conditions of the study).

---

### b) Approaches to ensuring internal validity (6 marks)

- **(i) Randomization:** **Random assignment** of participants to conditions so that extraneous variables are **distributed by chance** across groups. This **equates groups on average** and reduces selection bias, making it more plausible that any difference in the DV is due to the IV.

- **(ii) Control and experimental groups:** The **experimental group** receives the treatment (IV); the **control group** does not (or receives a placebo). **Comparing** the two groups on the DV allows the researcher to attribute differences to the IV, provided other factors are held constant or randomized.

---

### c) Advantages and disadvantages of three designs (10 marks)

- **(i) Post-test only (treatment, then measure DV)**  
  **Advantages:** Simple; no pre-test sensitization or practice effects.  
  **Disadvantages:** No baseline; cannot rule out pre-existing differences between groups unless randomization is used; weaker for inferring change.

- **(ii) Pre-test → treatment → post-test**  
  **Advantages:** Shows **change** within participants; can control for initial differences when analyzing gain scores or using pre-test as covariate.  
  **Disadvantages:** **Testing effect**: pre-test can affect post-test; **regression to the mean** if participants are selected on extreme pre-test scores.

- **(iii) Treatment group (treatment then measure DV) vs control group (no treatment, measure DV)**  
  **Advantages:** Direct **comparison** between treatment and no-treatment; with random assignment, strong support for **causal inference**; no pre-test needed.  
  **Disadvantages:** Requires enough participants for two groups; if assignment is not random, **selection** can confound results.

## Question 3 (20 marks) — LaTeX

### Part 1a) Use of Book, Article, and Report (3 marks)

- **Book:** Long documents with chapters (e.g. books, theses); supports \part, \chapter, two-sided layout.  
- **Article:** Short documents (e.g. papers, reports); uses \section, \subsection; one-column by default.  
- **Report:** Similar to article but with \chapter; often used for longer reports and theses.

---

### Part 1b) Can a document be created without a class? (2 marks)

**No.** Every LaTeX document must start with `\documentclass{...}`. The class defines the overall structure (sections, chapters, margins, etc.). Without it, LaTeX does not know how to build the document.

---

### Part 2) At least three advantages of LaTeX (3 marks)

- **Quality:** Professional typesetting (math, tables, references).  
- **Separation of content and layout:** Focus on content; layout is handled by the class and packages.  
- **Portability:** Plain text; works on many systems; easy version control.  
- **Consistency:** Numbering, cross-references, and bibliography are automated and consistent.

---

### Part 3) Role of packages and how to use them (3 marks)

- **Role:** Packages **extend** LaTeX (e.g. graphics, fonts, tables, citations). They add commands and environments without changing the core.

- **Use:** Load in the preamble with `\usepackage[options]{packagename}` (e.g. `\usepackage{graphicx}`). Options go in square brackets; multiple packages can be listed: `\usepackage{graphicx, amsmath}`.
