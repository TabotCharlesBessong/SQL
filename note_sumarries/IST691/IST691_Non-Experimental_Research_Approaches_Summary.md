## 1. Classifying Research

### 1.1 Experimental vs Descriptive (Non-Experimental) Research

| Type | Purpose | Key feature |
|------|--------|-------------|
| **Experimental** | Identify cause and effect | Researcher **manipulates** an independent variable and measures its effect on a dependent variable; supports cause–effect conclusions. |
| **Descriptive (Non-experimental)** | Describe phenomena | Researcher **observes** variables without manipulating them; **does not prove causation**. |

### 1.2 Quantitative vs Qualitative Research

| Aspect | Quantitative | Qualitative |
|--------|---------------|------------|
| **Data** | Numerical (statistics, measurements) | Non-numerical (text, interviews, observations) |
| **Focus** | Measure and test theories/hypotheses | Explore ideas, experiences, and meanings in depth |
| **Purpose** | Confirm theories, find patterns, generalize to a population | Understand concepts, motivations, emotions; rich context |
| **Methods** | Surveys, experiments, numerical observations | Interviews, focus groups, case studies |

**Choosing an approach:**
- **Qualitative:** When you need to explore a topic in depth or understand specific experiences or perspectives.
- **Quantitative:** When you need to measure something, test a hypothesis, or establish cause–effect relationships.
- **Mixed methods:** When the research question needs both numerical and in-depth data.

## 2. Nonexperimental Quantitative Research

Overview of common non-experimental quantitative designs:

- Correlational study  
- Ex post facto study  
- Longitudinal and cross-sectional studies  
- Naturalistic observation  
- Meta-analysis  
- Analysis of existing data  
- Survey research  

### 2.1 Correlational Study

- **Definition:** A non-experimental method where the researcher measures two (or more) variables and assesses the **statistical relationship** between them, without manipulating variables or controlling extraneous variables.
- **Aim:** Understand the **strength and direction** of the relationship.

**Types of correlation:**

| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|--------|
| **Positive** | As one variable increases, the other increases; as one decreases, the other decreases. | More wealth ↔ more cars owned. |
| **Negative** | As one variable increases, the other decreases, and vice versa. | Higher education ↔ lower crime rates (e.g. at country level). |
| **No correlation** | Change in one variable does not consistently relate to change in the other. | — |

**Important:** **Correlation does not imply causation.** For example, education and crime may both be related to a third factor (e.g. poverty) rather than one causing the other.

### 2.2 Ex Post Facto Study (After-the-Fact / Causal-Comparative / Quasi-Experimental)

- **Definition:** Examines cause–effect relationships between variables **without manipulating** them; the researcher selects or groups participants **after** the fact (e.g. after a characteristic or event has occurred).
- **Use:** When the independent variable **cannot be manipulated** (e.g. height, gender, past exposure).
- **Example:** Studying the relationship between height and IQ by forming groups based on height; height is not manipulated, only observed and used to form groups.

### 2.3 Longitudinal Study vs Cross-Sectional Study

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Longitudinal** | The **same** sample is measured on the same characteristics at **multiple time points** (repeated measures over time). |
| **Cross-sectional** | **Different** representative samples are measured **at one point in time** (snapshot comparison of groups or segments). |

### 2.4 Naturalistic Observation

- **Definition:** A descriptive technique for **unobtrusively** collecting data on behaviour as it occurs **in natural settings**, without manipulating or influencing variables.
- **Examples:** Usability research (observing users with a product); consumer research (e.g. shopping behaviour after a layout change); child development (e.g. language development at home via recordings).

### 2.5 Meta-Analysis

- **Definition:** A **quantitative** technique to **integrate and summarize** a large number of existing studies (e.g. effect sizes, significance).
- **Uses:** Resolve conflicting results across studies; obtain a more accurate **estimate of effect magnitude**; increase statistical power.

### 2.6 Analysis of Existing Data

- **Definition:** Using **already collected** datasets (e.g. administrative, survey, or archival data) to answer a research question.
- **Advantages:** Often more **cost-effective** and **efficient** than collecting new data.

### 2.7 Survey Research

- **Definition:** Non-experimental research that uses **interviews or questionnaires** to gather data on a given state of affairs in a **representative sample** of the population.
- Can be **quantitative** or **qualitative**.
- **Two important features:**
  1. Variables are usually measured via **self-reports** (questionnaires or interviews).
  2. Strong emphasis on **sampling**; survey researchers often prefer **large random samples** for accurate population estimates.

**Data collection strategies:**

| Strategy | Pros | Cons |
|----------|------|------|
| **In-person interviews** | Highest response rates; closest personal contact; good when judgments about respondents are needed (e.g. some mental health assessments) | Most costly |
| **Telephone** | Lower cost than in-person; some personal contact | Lower response rates; landline-based sampling frames are less representative now (many use only cell phones) |
| **Mail** | Lower cost | Often lowest response rates; more susceptible to non-response bias |
| **Internet** | Very common; easy to build and use; low cost; good when population has known email addresses (e.g. organization members) | Hard to get a complete sampling frame for many populations |

**Questionnaire construction:**
- Answers can be influenced by **wording**, **order of items**, **response options**, and other factors, which can add noise or create **systematic bias**.
- Challenges include: clarifying the question (e.g. what counts as “alcoholic drinks,” “typical day”); how respondents **retrieve** and **format** answers; and **editing** responses (e.g. social desirability).

**Question types:**

| Type | Description | Pros | Cons |
|------|-------------|------|------|
| **Open-ended** | Respondents answer in their own words; no fixed response set | Unbiased; no researcher-imposed expectations; good when possible responses are unknown | More likely to be skipped; harder to analyze (transcription, coding, qualitative analysis) |
| **Close-ended** | Respondents choose from a fixed set of options | Quick for respondents; easy to analyze (can be converted to numbers) | Must design appropriate options; more difficult to write well |

**Ordering of questions:**
- Put **most important** questions early (when respondent is more engaged).
- Put **sensitive** questions (e.g. attitudes, preferences) earlier to reduce conditioning by later items.
- Put **factual/historical** items later (when respondent may be more fatigued).

**Questionnaire length:**
- Affects **response rates**, **costs**, and **data quality**.
- **Longer** questionnaires: higher cost, more burden, often lower response rates and possibly lower quality.
- **Below a certain length**, small changes in length may have little effect on response rate or quality.

**Obtaining participants:**
- **Population:** The entire group about which you want to draw conclusions.
- **Sample:** The group from which you actually collect data (always smaller than the population).
- **Convenience (haphazard) sampling:** Non-probability; based on who is available; **not generalizable** to the population; used when few members of the target population are accessible.
- **Random sampling:** Every member of the population has an equal chance of being selected; **needed for generalizing** results to the population.

## 3. Qualitative Research

### 3.1 General Characteristics

- Investigates people in **natural environments** (in the field).
- **Interpretative:** Data (words, pictures, documents, etc.) require **interpretation**.
- **Multimethod:** Uses a **variety** of methods (e.g. personal accounts, introspection, individual interviews, observation, documents, history, photographs).
- Typically **does not test** hypotheses or prior theories; may **develop new theories** from the data.
- Generally **does not aim to generalize** findings to a large population.
- Often uses interviews, observations, and **content analysis** of existing materials (newspapers, books, videos, case records).
- Response categories are often **not** fixed in advance; they may emerge from the data.

### 3.2 Common Types of Qualitative Research

#### Grounded Theory (GT)

- **Focus:** **Generating theory from the data** (theory “grounded” in participants’ experiences).
- Used to uncover **social processes** (e.g. social relationships and group behaviours).
- **Process:** Collect and analyze data **simultaneously**; identify patterns; develop concepts and categories; build hypotheses or a new theory from emerging categories.
- **Contrast with traditional approach:** In GT, the researcher does not start with a fixed theoretical framework and hypotheses; theory emerges from the data rather than being tested by it.

#### Phenomenology

- **Focus:** **Lived experience** — the meaning and essence of a **particular phenomenon** as experienced by individuals.
- Involves **describing** individuals’ (or a group’s) **conscious experience** of a phenomenon.
- **Example:** Interviewing people who have experienced a major illness to understand their perceptions and feelings.

#### Ethnography

- **Focus:** **Scientific description** of peoples and cultures (customs, habits, differences).
- Involves **immersive fieldwork** and **participant observation** to understand the **culture** of a group.
- **Example:** Living in a community for an extended period to observe and document social structures, beliefs, and behaviours.
- Data from observations and interviews are used to draw conclusions about how societies and individuals function.

#### Case Study

- **Focus:** **In-depth** investigation of a **single** unit: one individual, group, event, or community.
- **Example:** In-depth study of a small business’s challenges during the pandemic, using interviews, internal documents, and observations.

### 3.3 Strengths and Weaknesses of Qualitative Research

| Strengths | Weaknesses |
|-----------|------------|
| **Depth of understanding** | **Subjectivity** in interpretation |
| **Flexibility** in design and process | **Suggestive**, not definitive |
| | **Limited generalizability** |

**Mixed methodology** (combining qualitative and quantitative approaches) is possible and often used when the research question benefits from both.
