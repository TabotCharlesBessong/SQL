# IST691: Variables Used in Research — Summary

Summary of the course material **Variables Used in Research** (Research Methodology and Scientific Writing).

---

## 1. Introduction

- Variables are identified **after** the research problem and hypothesis have been specified.
- **Variable:** A **measurable characteristic** that **changes or varies** (in research).

---

## 2. Types of Variables

### 2.1 Core Variables in Experiments

| Variable | Role |
|----------|------|
| **Independent Variable (IV)** | The factor the researcher **changes or manipulates** to observe its effect — the **“cause.”** |
| **Dependent Variable (DV)** | The **outcome or effect** that is **measured**; it changes in response to the IV — the **“effect.”** |
| **Control Variable** | Kept **constant** so it does not influence results; helps **isolate** the IV’s effect. |
| **Extraneous Variable** | Any variable **other than the IV** that could affect the DV. |
| **Confounding Variable** | An **unexpected** factor that affects **both** the IV and the DV, **distorting** the true relationship. |

### 2.2 Confounding vs Extraneous vs Control

- **Extraneous variables** may affect results and need to be considered or controlled.
- **Confounding variables** are **uncontrolled** factors that influence both the IV and the DV; they are the **biggest threat to validity** because they provide an **alternative explanation** for the results.
- **Control variables** are **actively managed** (held constant) so they do not affect results, allowing only the IV’s impact to be measured.

### 2.3 Qualitative vs Quantitative Variables

| Type | Description | Examples |
|------|-------------|----------|
| **Qualitative (categorical)** | Varies in **kind**; non-numerical, descriptive. | Gender, eye colour, treatment type, hair colour (e.g. Black, Brown, Red). |
| **Quantitative** | **Numerical**; varies in **amount**. | Age, height, test scores, loudness (decibels), latency (time). |
| ↳ **Discrete** | Countable; **whole numbers**. | Number of children. |
| ↳ **Continuous** | Can take **any value** within a range. | Height, temperature. |

### 2.4 Moderating vs Intervening (Mediating) Variables

| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|--------|
| **Moderating variable** | **Influences the strength or direction** of the relationship between the IV and the DV. | Age moderates the link between exercise (IV) and weight loss (DV) — exercise may be more effective for younger people. Mental health may moderate how sleep affects grades. |
| **Intervening (mediating) variable** | Explains the **process or mechanism** through which the IV affects the DV (IV → Mediator → DV). | Education (IV) → better job skills (mediator) → higher income (DV). Sleep quality (IV) → alertness (mediator) → academic success (DV). |

## 3. The Independent Variable (IV)

### 3.1 In Non-Experimental Research

- There is **no manipulation** of the IV.
- Researchers **observe and measure** variables as they **naturally occur**, identifying **predictors or correlates** rather than causes.
- The IV is typically a **characteristic** (e.g. age, gender, income) studied for its **relationship** to the DV, without controlling or altering it — focus is on **associations**, not cause-and-effect.
- **Example:** Observing how naturally occurring income relates to education, without manipulating income.

### 3.2 In Experimental Research

- The IV is the factor the researcher **deliberately changes, manipulates, or controls** to observe its effect on the DV — the **“cause”** in a cause–effect relationship.

### 3.3 Ways to Achieve Variation in the IV

| Strategy | Description | Example |
|----------|-------------|--------|
| **Presence vs absence** | One group receives the **treatment**, the other does not; compare the two groups. | One group gets a drug, the other a placebo; compare on some measure. |
| **Amount of variable** | **Different amounts** of the variable are administered to different groups. | Different dosages. |
| **Type of variable** | **Different kinds or categories** of the variable are compared. | Different teaching methods, different interfaces. |

### 3.4 Establishing Variation: Two Main Ways

| Method | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Experimental manipulation** | The experimenter **administers** different specific levels of the variable to different groups. |
| **Individual difference manipulation** | The researcher **selects** participants who already **differ** on a measured internal state (e.g. self-esteem, anxiety) and then looks for effects of that difference. |

### 3.5 Experimental Manipulation: Two Common Approaches

| Approach | Description | Notes |
|----------|-------------|--------|
| **Instructional manipulation** | The IV is varied by giving **different instructions** to different groups. | **Risks:** Some participants may be inattentive; instructions may be interpreted differently. **Minimize:** Keep instructions simple, give them emphatically, and relate them to the task. |
| **Event manipulation** | The IV is varied by **altering the events** participants experience. | **Example:** Different drug dosages in drug research. |

### 3.6 Constructing (Operationally Defining) the IV

- The researcher must decide **how the IV is constructed** — i.e. how it is **operationally defined** (the exact empirical operations).
- **Concrete variables:** Easier (e.g. “administer 2 mg of aspirin,” “display words for 2 seconds”).
- **Abstract constructs** (e.g. attitude, anxiety, learning): Harder because there is no single definition or empirical referent; **review how the variable is defined in previous research**; sometimes several techniques exist and the choice of which to use is the main difficulty.

### 3.7 Construct Validity of the IV

- **Construct validity:** The extent to which the **abstract construct** (conceptual variable) of interest can be **inferred from** the operational definition.
- **Questions to ask:**
  - Do the experimental operations **represent** the abstract concept we intend, and do they behave as theory predicts?
  - Will different **levels** of the IV make participants behave **differently**? In what ways?
- This ensures the IV reflects the **underlying theoretical idea**, so effects on the DV are due to the real concept, not a flawed operationalization or other factors.

### 3.8 Guidelines for Construct Validity of the IV

1. **Clear definition** of the abstract construct.
2. **Obtain data** showing that the empirical representation of the IV **produces the expected outcome** (e.g. if “hunger” is the construct, hungry rats should consume more food).
3. **Obtain data** showing that the empirical representation **does not vary** with measures of **related but different** constructs (e.g. “communication expertise” should relate to knowledge, not to power, position, or trustworthiness).

### 3.9 Number of Independent Variables

- There may be **one or more** IVs.
- If more than one: consider **interaction** — the **differential effect** that one IV has **at each level** of one or more other IVs.
- There is no theoretical limit, but **practical limits** apply.

### 3.10 Limiting the Number of IVs

| Perspective | Concern |
|-------------|---------|
| **Participant** | More variables → more tasks, events, tests → risk of **boredom, irritation, or resentment** → may introduce **confounding**. |
| **Researcher** | More IVs → **harder to interpret** the data. |
| **Rule of thumb** | The experiment should be **only as complex as necessary** for the important relationships to emerge clearly. **Do not** use a “why not?” approach (adding a variable because there is no reason not to). **Include only** variables that are important for revealing the relationships of interest. |

## 4. The Dependent Variable (DV)

### 4.1 In Non-Experimental Research

- The DV is the **outcome or response** variable that is **observed** as it naturally changes in relation to other (independent or predictor) variables that are also naturally occurring.
- It is the **effect or result** being studied (e.g. customer satisfaction, stress levels, health outcomes) when **direct manipulation** is not possible or ethical.

### 4.2 In Experimental Research

- The DV is the variable **defined to measure the effect** of the variation of the IV.
- **Choosing a measure** can be difficult. **Example:** Split-brain surgery was long thought to leave the person unchanged because personality/behaviour seemed little altered; when the **test environment and specific DVs** (e.g. short-term memory) were varied, significant effects of the surgery were demonstrated.

### 4.3 Task of the Dependent Variable

- **Determine** whether the IV **did or did not** produce an effect.
- If an effect was produced, the DV should:
  - Indicate whether the effect was **facilitating** or **inhibiting**.
  - **Reveal the magnitude** of the effect.
- The researcher must choose the **specific measure(s)** used to assess the effect of the IV.

### 4.4 Reliability and Validity of the DV

| Concept | Meaning |
|---------|--------|
| **Reliability (consistency)** | The extent to which **the same results** are obtained when the measure is used at **different times** under the **same conditions**. |
| **Validity (accuracy)** | The extent to which the DV **accurately reflects** the concept it is supposed to measure. |
| **Relationship** | A measure can be **reliable but not valid** (consistent but wrong). A **valid** measure is generally **reliable** (accurate results tend to be reproducible). |

### 4.5 Reliability of the DV in Practice

- If participants respond **consistently** on the measure, the DV is reliable.
- Many experiments are **single-occasion** — the DV is observed **only once**, so **reliability cannot be assessed** within that study.
- **Aggregating** responses over time tends to **increase** reliability.
- **Replication** of results across studies is a practical indicator of reliability: if an effect replicates, the DV measure is likely reliable; replication is unlikely if the measure is unreliable.
- **Conclusion:** We do not get reliability from a single study; if a **body of studies** gives the **same results**, the dependent variable measure can be considered reliable.

### 4.6 Validity of the DV

- **Key question:** What evidence is there that the DV **actually measures** the construct we want it to measure?
- **Example:** In a learning experiment, does **latency of response** really represent **degree of learning**?

### 4.7 Guidelines for Construct Validity of the DV

1. **Clear definition** of the construct.
2. **Obtain convergent and divergent data** to support that the construct has been measured:
   - **Convergent:** **Agreement** across **several measures** of the **same** construct (e.g. high correlation between two different measures of depression by different researchers suggests they are measuring the same thing).
   - **Divergent:** The DV is **not related** to **conceptually different** constructs (e.g. a measure of depression should not correlate highly with a measure of intelligence, because they are different concepts).

### 4.8 Number of Dependent Variables

- **Often:** When more than one DV could be used, the researcher **selects one** and proceeds.
- **When the field is not fully advanced:** It may be useful to use **more than one** DV.
- **Correlation between DVs** should be examined:
  - **High correlation:** They are likely measuring the **same** thing; all but one can be **dropped**.
  - **Low correlation:** May reflect **unreliability** of the measures, or that they are measuring **different aspects** of the construct.
