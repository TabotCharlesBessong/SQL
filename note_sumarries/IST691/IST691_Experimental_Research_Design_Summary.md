## 2. Introduction: Research Design

### 2.1 What Is a Research Design?

- **Research design:** The **outline, plan, or strategy** used to investigate a research problem.
- **Purpose:** To provide a plan for **answering the research questions**.

### 2.2 Factors to Consider

- Type of **data** to be collected  
- Nature of the **data** to be analyzed  
- **Research questions** to be answered  
- **Research goal or objective**  
- **Important:** The design should **maximize control** over factors that could interfere with the **validity** of the findings.

---

## 3. Faulty Research Designs

Designs that do **not** allow unambiguous conclusions about the effect of the treatment.

### 3.1 One-Group After-Only Design (One-Group Post-test Only)

- **Structure:** The outcome is measured **only once**, **after** exposing a **non-random** group to an intervention.
- **Characteristics:** **No control group**; **no measurement before** the intervention.
- **Example:** After a training programme, performance is measured; good performance is taken as success. **Problem:** Without a **basis for comparison**, we cannot tell whether performance was due to the programme or to other factors (maturation, prior ability, etc.). The design is **not useful** for establishing cause.

### 3.2 One-Group Before-After (Pretest-Post-test) Design

- **Structure:** A **treatment** is given **between** a **pre-test** and a **post-test** of the dependent variable.
- **Improvement over after-only:** We at least see change over time.
- **Flaw:** There is **no control group** that does **not** receive the treatment. So we cannot tell whether change is due to the treatment or to other factors (e.g. **maturation**, practice, history). Especially important when time passes between pretest and posttest.

### 3.3 Non-Equivalent Post-test-Only Design

- **Aim:** Improve on the above by adding a **comparison group**: one group receives the treatment (X), the other does not; both are compared on the dependent variable (Y).
- **Flaw:** The **control group is non-equivalent** — the two groups are **not** randomly formed. We **assume** they are equated on other variables, but even **matching** on age, gender, IQ, etc. **does not guarantee** equivalence. The **only** way to have reasonable assurance of equivalence is **random assignment** to experimental and control groups. When random assignment is **not possible**, matching is the “next best” option, but the design remains weak for causal inference.

## 4. Requirements of True Research Designs

A design should satisfy three criteria:

### 4.1 Criterion 1: Does the Design Answer the Research Question or Adequately Test the Hypothesis?

- The researcher must be able to say what **conclusions** can be drawn from the experiment.
- The **design** determines which **statistical tests** can be used, and those in turn determine what **conclusions** are possible.

### 4.2 Criterion 2: Have Extraneous Variables Been Controlled? (Internal Validity)

- To achieve **internal validity**, we must **rule out rival hypotheses** (alternative explanations for the result).
- This is achieved through:
  1. **Control techniques** (e.g. randomization — the main way to control **unknown** variables; matching, counterbalancing, etc., as in the “Control in Experimentation” material).
  2. A **control group** (see below).

**Control group:** The group that serves as a **standard of comparison** to determine whether the treatment had any effect. It does **not** receive the treatment (or receives a standard/placebo condition).

**Experimental group:** The group that **receives** some level of the independent variable (treatment).

**Why a control group:** (1) Provides **comparison**; (2) helps **control rival hypotheses**. All variables other than the one manipulated should operate **identically** on both groups so that the influence of extraneous variables is **held constant**. Participants in the control group should be **similar** to those in the experimental group; in theory, both groups would yield **identical** scores in the **absence** of the independent variable.

### 4.3 Criterion 3: Generalizability (External Validity)

- **Question:** Can the results be **applied** to individuals other than those in the experiment? If yes, **to whom** (e.g. only females, only college students)?
- **Note:** All studies should meet **criterion 1**. The **degree** to which criteria 2 and 3 are met **varies**. **Basic research** often emphasizes **internal validity**; **applied research** often cares about both internal and **external validity** because results are meant to be applied to people and institutions.

## 5. Pretesting Participants

### 5.1 What Is Pretesting?

- **Pretesting** is one way to obtain information about participants’ **state before** the treatment. The experimenter can then **observe change** in behaviour as a result of the treatment.
- **Question:** Is pretesting **essential** when we already use **random assignment** and compare experimental and control groups? Not always, but it is often useful.

### 5.2 Reasons to Pretest

| Reason | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **1) Increased sensitivity** | Matching participants on relevant variables (which **requires** pretesting) can increase the sensitivity of the design to detect treatment effects. |
| **2) Ceiling (or floor) effect** | By chance, participants might already hold **extreme** positions that the treatment cannot change. Pretesting reveals this. |
| **3) Initial position** | Some experiments need to know participants’ **initial level** on the DV because it may **interact** with the experimental condition. |
| **4) Initial comparability** | Random assignment is used to make groups comparable, but it is **not infallible**. Comparing **pretest means** across groups can reveal whether they are comparable. |
| **5) Evidence of change** | Pretest–posttest comparison gives **direct empirical evidence** that the treatment produced change in participants. |

## 6. True Research Designs

### 6.1 Definition and Types

- **True experimental design:** A design in which the influence of **extraneous variables** is **controlled** while the influence of the **independent variable** is **tested**.
- **Two broad types:** **After-only** design and **Before-after** design. The document focuses on **after-only** variants.

**Design structure** depends on: number of IVs; number of **levels** of each IV; whether **the same** or **different** participants are used in each condition → **within-participants** vs **between-participants** design.

---

## 7. After-Only Design (Post-test Only Control Group Design)

- **Idea:** Compare **post-test** scores of **experimental** and **control** groups to assess the effect of the treatment. No pretest (or pretest not used in the main comparison).
- **Between-participants:** Different participants in each condition. **Within-participants:** Same participants in all conditions (repeated measures).

### 7.1 Between-Participants After-Only Design

- Participants are **randomly assigned** to experimental and control groups.
- **Difference from faulty design:** Unlike the **non-equivalent** post-test-only design, groups are **randomly** formed, so we have greater assurance of equivalence.

### 7.2 Matched Between-Participants After-Only Design

- Because randomization is **not infallible**, participants can be **matched** on relevant variables **before** random assignment to **increase sensitivity** and improve group equivalence.
- **Two main extensions** of the between-participants after-only design:
  - **Simple randomized participants design**
  - **Factorial design**

### 7.3 Simple Randomized Participants Design

- A **between-participants** design that investigates **several levels** of the **one** independent variable (e.g. different doses of a drug, each assigned to a different group).

### 7.4 Factorial Design

- **Definition:** A **between-participants** design that allows investigation of both the **independent** and **interactive** influences of **more than one** independent variable.
- **Example:** Two IVs — A (e.g. 3 levels: A1, A2, A3) and B (e.g. 2 levels: B1, B2) → **6 combinations** (A1B1, A1B2, A2B1, A2B2, A3B1, A3B2). Each combination is a **cell** (a specific treatment condition).

**Two types of effects:**

| Effect | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Main effect** | The **overall** effect of **one** independent variable on the DV, **averaging across** all levels of the other factor(s). It is the direct, independent impact of that factor. In the example, main effect of A: do the three A means differ? Main effect of B: do the two B means differ? |
| **Interaction effect** | The effect of **one** IV **depends on** the **level** of another IV (they “work together” rather than only independently). E.g. the effect of A may differ for B1 vs B2. |

**Interpreting main effects and interaction:**

- When **only** a main effect **or** only an interaction is significant → interpret that effect.
- When **both** main effects **and** interaction are significant → the main effect is **contained in** (qualified by) the interaction. **Interpret the interaction**; the interaction gives the correct meaning rather than the main effect alone.
- **Classic interaction pattern (e.g. diagram (c)):** Column means (A) and row means (B) may be identical (no main effects), but the **effect of A** is **opposite** for B1 vs B2 — so A is effective, but its **direction** depends on B. **Parallel lines** (in a plot of means) typically indicate **no interaction**; **non-parallel** lines suggest possible interaction.

### 7.5 Within-Participants After-Only Design (Repeated Measures)

- The **same** participants are **repeatedly** measured on the DV after participating in **all** experimental treatment conditions.

**Advantages:**

1. **Equivalence:** The same people are in every condition, so they serve as their **own control**; variables like age, gender, prior experience are **constant** across conditions.
2. **Fewer participants:** Only as many participants as in **one** group of a between-participants design (per condition).

**Disadvantages:**

1. **Sequencing effects:** Order and carry-over effects can **confound** results (same participants do all conditions in some order).
2. If sequencing effects are **not linear**, **counterbalancing** may not fully remove their confounding influence.
3. Because disadvantages often **outweigh** advantages, the **within-participants after-only** design is **not commonly** used; **between-participants** designs are preferred when possible.
