## 2. Introduction to Ethics (General)

### 2.1 What Is Ethics?

- **Ethics** (moral philosophy) is a branch of philosophy that involves **systematizing, defending, and recommending** concepts of **right and wrong** behaviour.

### 2.2 Views of Ethics

| View | Description | Examples / notes |
|------|-------------|------------------|
| **Rules for right and wrong** | Standards that distinguish right from wrong. | Golden Rule; Hippocratic Oath (“First, do no harm”); religious codes (e.g. Ten Commandments). **Problem:** People may share some norms but **interpret, apply, and balance** them differently (e.g. agree murder is wrong but disagree on abortion). |
| **Disciplines that study conduct** | Focus on fields that study standards of conduct. | Philosophy, theology, law, psychology, sociology. **Example:** Medical ethicist studies ethical standards in medicine. |
| **Method or perspective for deciding how to act** | Ethics as a way to decide how to act and to analyze complex problems. | E.g. global warming: economist looks at costs/benefits of policies; environmental ethicist examines ethical values and principles. |

### 2.3 Ethics in Professions and Research

- Different **disciplines, institutions, and professions** have **standards** that fit their aims; these help members **coordinate** actions and build **public trust** (e.g. medicine, law, engineering, business).
- **Research ethics** applies to people who conduct **scientific research** or other **scholarly or creative** work. **Research ethics** is the discipline that studies these **norms**.

## 3. Introduction to Ethics in Research

### 3.1 What Are Research Ethics?

- **Research ethics:** The **moral principles and codes of conduct** that guide how studies are **designed, executed, and reported** to ensure **integrity** and the **protection of participants**.

### 3.2 Objectives of Research Ethics

1. **Protect** human and animal participants from harm.  
2. **Ensure** data reliability and scientific integrity.  
3. **Maintain** public trust in scientific progress.

---

## 4. Why Worry About Ethics in Research?

### 4.1 Not Only About “Bad People”

- Research ethics is **not only** (or even mainly) about bad people doing bad things.
- It is also about **imperfect people** doing **imperfect things** for many reasons, including:
  - **Socially acceptable** practice that is later deemed wrong.
  - **Accident.**
  - **Misdemeanour** — minor wrongdoing that is rationalized (e.g. taking shortcuts).
  - **Missing something**, especially with new methods or technology.
  - Being involved through **others’ wrongs**.
  - Situations where **the right thing is unclear**.
  - **Professional pressures:** “Publish or perish,” tenure, keeping up with peers, securing grants, being first to a discovery, etc.

### 4.2 Positive Foundations

- **Doing good** for humans, animals, the planet, future generations through the **pursuit of truth and knowledge**.
- **Duty to respect individuals.**
- **Possible duties to animals.**
- **Obligations to society.**
- Various obligations flow from these; they can **conflict** with one another.

---

## 5. Why It Is Important to Adhere to Ethical Norms in Research

1. **Norms promote the aims of research** (e.g. knowledge, truth, avoidance of error).  
   - Example: Prohibitions against **fabricating, falsifying, or misrepresenting** data promote truth and reduce error.

2. **Ethical standards promote values essential to collaboration:** trust, accountability, mutual respect, fairness.  
   - Research often involves cooperation across disciplines and institutions.  
   - **Examples:** Authorship guidelines, copyright/patenting, data sharing, confidentiality protect interests while encouraging collaboration; researchers want credit and protection from stolen or premature disclosure of ideas.

3. **Norms help ensure researchers can be held accountable** to **funders**.

4. **Ethical norms help build public support** for research (e.g. willingness to fund projects when the quality and integrity of research are trusted).

5. **Norms promote other important moral and social values:** e.g. social responsibility, human rights, animal welfare, compliance with law, public health and safety.  
   - **Ethical lapses can cause serious harm:** e.g. fabricated data in a clinical trial may harm or kill patients; ignoring radiation or biosafety rules may endanger the researcher, staff, and students.

## 6. Foundational Principles

### 6.1 Respect for Persons (Autonomy)

- Treat individuals as **independent agents** and **protect** those with **diminished autonomy**.
- **Voluntary participation:** Participants must be **free** to join or leave at any time without penalty.
- **Informed consent:** Participants must **understand** purpose, risks, and benefits before agreeing.
- **Protection of vulnerable groups:** Extra safeguards for children, those with diminished capacity, or those in compromised positions.

### 6.2 Beneficence and Non-Maleficence

- **Do no harm:** Minimize **physical, psychological, social, and legal** risks.
- **Maximize benefit:** Research should provide **value** that **outweighs** its risks.

### 6.3 Justice

- **Fair distribution** of the **burdens and benefits** of research.
- **Avoid exploiting** “convenient” or vulnerable populations (e.g. marginalized groups).
- **Ensure** results **benefit** the communities involved.

## 7. Other Core Issues

### 7.1 Integrity and Honesty in Scientific Practice

- Researchers must **truthfully** report data and results — no **fabrication**, **falsification**, or **plagiarism**.

### 7.2 Avoiding Research Misconduct

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Fabrication** | **Inventing** data or results. |
| **Falsification** | **Manipulating** research materials or **omitting** findings to fit a hypothesis. |
| **Plagiarism** | **Using** others’ work or ideas **without credit**. |

### 7.3 Responsible Publication

- Advance **scholarship**, not only career.
- **Avoid** duplicative (redundant) publications.

### 7.4 “The Truth, the Whole Truth, and Nothing but the Truth”

- **The truth:** Be **honest** about your research.
- **The whole truth:** **Omitting** parts of findings may be misconduct or violate other moral norms.
- **Nothing but the truth:** Do not **overstate** results, add **irrelevant or misleading** information, or **exaggerate** significance.

### 7.5 Privacy, Confidentiality, and Anonymity

| Term | Meaning |
|------|--------|
| **Privacy** | Protecting participants’ private sphere. |
| **Anonymity** | Data collected **without** identifying information; **even the researcher** cannot identify the participant. |
| **Confidentiality** | The researcher **knows** identity but **keeps it private** (no public disclosure). |
| **Data protection** | **Secure storage** of data (e.g. encrypted digital files). |

## 8. Ethical Dilemmas

- In some fields (e.g. psychology, medicine), research can create **special dilemmas**.
- **Tension:** Obligation to **conduct** research vs. exposing participants to **stress, pain, aggression, deception**.
- **Ethical dilemma:** The researcher’s **conflict** in weighing the **potential cost to participants** against the **potential gain** from the research.
- **Guidance:** **Primary consideration** should be the **welfare of the participant**.  
- Because this judgment is **subjective**, it **should not be made solely** by the researcher and colleagues — hence the need for **independent review** (e.g. IRB).

---

## 9. Institutional Oversight

### 9.1 Institutional Review Board (IRB)

- **Role:** Most **human-subject research** requires **prior approval** from an IRB (research ethics committee).
- **Function:** Protects human participants through **advance and periodic independent review** of the ethical acceptability of proposals.
- **Responsibilities:** Review **before** initiation **all** research involving human participants; protect **welfare, rights, and privacy** of human subjects.
- **Powers:** Can **approve, exempt, disapprove, monitor**, and **require modifications** for research within its jurisdiction.
- **Continuing review:** IRB monitors **ongoing** studies for “**mission creep**” (going beyond stated objectives).
- **Conflict of interest (COI):** Researchers must **disclose** financial or personal interests that could **influence** findings.

---

## 10. Codes and Policies for Research Ethics

- Given the importance of ethics in research, many **professional associations**, **government agencies**, and **universities** have adopted **codes, rules, and policies** on research ethics.
- Researchers are expected to know and follow the relevant codes and institutional policies.
