## 2. Causation

### 2.1 Meaning

- **Causation** means that the **manipulation** of one event **causes** another event.
- The **experimental research approach** is the scientific way to study such cause–effect relationships: the researcher **manipulates** one or more **independent variables** and **observes** their impact on **dependent variables** to identify cause-and-effect relationships.

### 2.2 Why Causation Is Tricky

- Several alternative explanations can exist for the same outcome.  
- **Example:** A child stops colouring the wall after being beaten. Did they stop because of the beating, because they were tired, or because their attention shifted? Without control and design, we cannot conclude that the beating *caused* the stop.

### 2.3 Example: History of Attempts to Find the Cause of Malaria

- **Medieval view:** “Bad air” in lowlands was thought to cause malaria → towns built on hilltops. The disease did not disappear but was reduced.
- **Later:** Quinine helped patients; the **malaria parasite** was found in blood and was seen as the cause. Malaria remained a public health problem.
- **Later:** The parasite was found to be **transmitted by certain mosquitoes** → the mosquito was treated as the cause, and control efforts targeted it.
- **Current picture:** Malaria has disappeared from many rich northern areas even though mosquitoes and infected people are still present. The document suggests that malaria may depend on a **balanced set of social, economic, and biological factors** — i.e. causation is complex and context-dependent.

**Lesson:** Identifying a single “cause” is difficult; what we often find is **covariation** (e.g. bad air, parasite, mosquito each covaried with malaria at different times). A full explanation may require a **system of interrelated conditions**, not one necessary-and-sufficient cause.

## 3. Necessary and Sufficient Conditions

### 3.1 Definitions

| Type | Meaning | Example |
|------|--------|--------|
| **Necessary condition** | Must be **present** for the effect to occur, but is **not enough by itself**. | To become an alcoholic, one must consume alcohol. Oxygen is necessary for fire. |
| **Sufficient condition** | **Always produces** the effect when present; the effect is **guaranteed**, but there may be **other ways** for the effect to occur. | Destroying the auditory nerve always causes loss of hearing (but hearing can be lost in other ways). Breaking a leg is sufficient for pain (but pain can occur in other ways). |

### 3.2 Key Difference

- **Necessary:** Required, but not enough on its own.  
- **Sufficient:** Enough to produce the effect, but not the only way.  
- **Necessary and sufficient:** The condition is the **only** way and **guarantees** the effect.

### 3.3 Cause as Necessary and Sufficient

- For a condition to qualify as **the cause** of an event:
  - The effect would **never** occur **unless** the condition were present (**necessary**).
  - **Whenever** the condition is present, the effect would occur (**sufficient**).
- If only **sufficient:** the effect can happen in other ways (e.g. other causes of hearing loss).  
- If only **necessary:** the effect may not happen even when the condition is present (e.g. not everyone who drinks alcohol becomes an alcoholic).  
- Once a **necessary and sufficient** cause is found, we have an isolated, complete explanation that (in principle) does not change — unlike the evolving malaria story.

### 3.4 Falsification

- **One view:** Confirming an experiment only means the hypothesis **survived** the test. If the experiment **fails** to confirm a prediction or theory, that prediction or theory is **falsified**.  
- **Position of falsification:** The best we can say about a theory or prediction is that it is **not yet falsified** — we cannot prove it true, only fail to disprove it.

### 3.5 Alternative View: Network of Causes

- Another view sees **causation as a network** of cause–effect relations.  
- In the malaria example, several factors **covaried** with malaria (bad air, parasite, mosquito); each was at some point treated as *the* cause.  
- This view holds that an effect often requires a **balanced system of interrelated conditions** (e.g. mosquitoes, parasites, and specific social and economic conditions together).  
- It may be **hard to find** necessary-and-sufficient conditions even when many studies point in that direction.

## 4. What Is an Experiment?

### 4.1 Definition

- An **experiment** is an **objective observation** of phenomena that are **made to occur** in a **strictly controlled situation** in which:
  - **One or more factors are varied** (the independent variable(s)), and  
  - **Other factors are kept constant.**  
- It is **designed to answer** whether there is a **causal relationship** between two variables — i.e. whether changes in the **independent variable** **cause** changes in the **dependent variable**.

### 4.2 Two Fundamental Features

1. **Manipulation of the independent variable**  
   The researcher **manipulates** (systematically varies) the level of the independent variable. The different levels are called **conditions**.

2. **Control of extraneous variables**  
   The researcher **controls** (minimizes the variability of) variables other than the independent and dependent variable. These other variables are called **extraneous variables**.

---

## 5. Advantages of the Experimental Approach

- **Inferring causal relationships:** Design allows stronger conclusions about cause and effect than non-experimental designs.  
- **Precise manipulation:** The experimenter can **manipulate precisely** one or more variables of their choosing.  
- **Usefulness:** Has produced results that:
  - Have **lasted over time**
  - Have **suggested new studies**
  - Have **suggested solutions to practical problems**

---

## 6. Disadvantages (Criticisms) of the Experimental Approach

- **Artificiality (especially in the lab):** Findings are often obtained in **laboratory conditions** (artificial, sterile atmosphere) that differ greatly from **real-life** situations, which can limit generalizability (external validity).  
- **Design and time:** Experiments can be **difficult to design** and may require **a long time** to plan and conduct.

---

## 7. Experimental Research Settings

### 7.1 Field Experimentation

- Experiments carried out in a **natural setting** (outside the laboratory).  
- **Advantage:** **Not subject to the same artificiality** as the lab; setting is closer to real life.  
- **Disadvantage:** **Control of extraneous variables** is typically **not as good** as in the laboratory.

### 7.2 Laboratory Experimentation

- Experiments carried out in a **laboratory setting**.  
- **Advantage:** The experimenter can **control and eliminate** the influence of **extraneous variables** to a high degree.  
- **Disadvantage:** The **price** of this control is the **artificiality** of the situation.

### 7.3 Trade-off

| Setting   | Control over extraneous variables | Artificiality / realism      |
|----------|-----------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| **Lab**  | High                              | More artificial               |
| **Field**| Lower                             | More natural, closer to life  |
