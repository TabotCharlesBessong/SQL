## 2. Sources of Research Ideas

Research ideas can come from:

| Source | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Everyday life** | Daily experiences that suggest a need for research (e.g. improving software, user interfaces, equipment; using data for management and decision-making). |
| **Practical issues** | Problems faced by organizations such as businesses, hospitals, etc. |
| **Past research** | Existing studies answer some questions but often **raise new questions** based on their findings. |
| **Theory** | See below. |

### 2.1 Theory as a Source

- **Theory:** A group of **logically organized** (deductively related) laws.
- Theory is both a **goal** and a **tool**:
  - **As a goal:** Theories **summarize and integrate** existing knowledge.
  - **As a tool:** Theories **guide research**; a good theory also suggests **new relationships** and **new predictions**, so it acts as a **source of researchable ideas**.

## 3. Review of Literature

### 3.1 Why Review Literature?

- A literature review is needed **before and after** obtaining a research topic.
- Reviewing early in the process can:
  - Show whether the research question **has already been answered**
  - Help **evaluate how interesting** the question is
  - Give **ideas on how to conduct** your study
  - Show **how your study fits** into existing research

### 3.2 What Counts as Research Literature?

- **Research literature** = all **published research** in the field.
- **Generally not included** (and often considered unreliable for scientific research): self-help books, dictionary and encyclopedia entries, general websites, and similar sources aimed at the public — they are usually **not peer-reviewed** and may rely on common sense or personal experience.
- **Wikipedia:** May contain useful information, but **anonymous authors**, possible lack of expertise, and **continually changing content** make it **unsuitable as a basis for sound scientific research**.
- **Good sources:** Academic **journal articles**, **books**, **conference proceedings**, association papers, **dissertations**, etc., written by scholars and researchers for scholars and researchers.

### 3.3 Starting the Literature Review

- Be familiar with the **library** (and library systems).
- Be prepared to spend **considerable time** searching.
- Use **abstracts** to decide whether an article is worth reading in full.
- For articles you read in full, take **detailed notes**: complete reference, methodology, important findings, strengths, weaknesses, and your own comments.
- Use **electronic citation and reference tools** to manage references.

### 3.4 Defining Objectives

- Clarify **why** you are doing the search (e.g. to familiarize yourself with the topic, to develop a methodology).
- Defining objectives **focuses** the literature search.

### 3.5 Doing the Search: Sources

- **Books** — Good place to start; provide an introduction and a summary of published work up to the time of writing.
- **Journals and conference proceedings** — See below.
- **Electronic databases** — e.g. those available through the university library.
- **Internet resources** — Email, discussion groups, web searches (especially of **academic** sites).

### 3.6 Journal Articles vs Conference Papers

| | Journal article | Conference paper |
|--|----------------|------------------|
| **Length** | Longer | Generally short |
| **Style** | Definite structure | Often more concise |
| **Publication** | Published in journals | Presented at a conference |

### 3.7 Types of Articles in Professional Journals

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Empirical research reports** | Describe **new empirical study(ies)** by the authors: research question, why it is interesting, previous research, **method**, **results**, conclusions. |
| **Review articles** | **Summarize** previously published research on a topic and often present **new ways** to organize or explain results. |
| **Theoretical article** | A review article focused mainly on presenting a **new theory**. |

## 4. Formulating the Research Problem

### 4.1 Research Problem

- The researcher must state **clearly** the research problem.
- **Research problem:** An **exact statement** of the specific problem to be investigated — a specific issue, **gap in knowledge**, or area of concern that the study aims to investigate, solve, or explain. It forms the **foundation** for research questions and objectives.
- The research problem must be **identified before any data are collected**.

### 4.2 Research Question

- **Research question:** The **“how”** — the **specific, focused inquiry** derived from the research problem that guides exactly what you will study to address it.
- It is an **interrogative sentence or statement** that asks what **relationship** exists between **two or more variables**.

### 4.3 Three Criteria for a Good Research Question

1. **There should be a relationship between the variables**  
   The question should clearly express a **link, association, or cause–effect relationship** between two or more variables.

2. **The problem should be stated in question form (or as a clear, unambiguous statement)**  
   Present it as an interrogative sentence or a clear, specific declarative statement (e.g. when stating a hypothesis) to **minimize misinterpretation**.

3. **The problem statement should imply possibilities of empirical testing**  
   The variables and the proposed relationship must be **measurable** in the real world (through data collection and analysis). This distinguishes a **researchable** problem from a purely philosophical or moral question.

### 4.4 Specificity of the Research Question

- **Specificity** = how precisely the research question is stated.
- **Too vague (example):** “What effect does the environment have on learning ability?” — Meets basic criteria but is not specific enough.
- **More specific (example):** “What effect does the amount of exposure to words have on the speed with which they are learned?”
- **How specific?** Specific enough for the researcher to:
  - Have a **clear grasp** of the variables to be investigated, and
  - (In an experiment) **design and carry out** the experiment effectively.

## 5. Formulating Hypotheses

### 5.1 What Is a Hypothesis?

- A **specific, testable prediction** about the **relationship between variables**.
- A **tentative answer** to a research question.
- **Grounded in existing knowledge** (theory, prior research).
- Formulated so it can be **supported or refuted** by data; often expressed as **“if–then”** statements linking independent and dependent variables.

### 5.2 Characteristics of a Good Hypothesis

| Characteristic | Meaning |
|----------------|---------|
| **Testable & falsifiable** | It must be possible to collect data to **prove or disprove** it. |
| **Specific & clear** | It **identifies the variables** (independent and dependent) and the **expected relationship**. |
| **Educated guess** | Based on **prior theories and knowledge**, not a random guess. |
| **Predictive** | It **states the expected outcome** of the study. |
| **Often directional** | It may **suggest the direction** of the relationship (e.g. “higher grades,” “faster recovery”). |

### 5.3 Types of Hypotheses in Statistical Testing

| Type | Notation | Description |
|------|----------|-------------|
| **Null hypothesis** | **H₀** | States that there is **no relationship or difference** between the variables. |
| **Scientific (alternative) hypothesis** | **Hₐ** | States the **researcher’s prediction** of a relationship or effect (what they expect to find). |

- In an experimental study, it is the **null hypothesis**, not the scientific hypothesis, that is **tested** statistically.
- The scientific hypothesis usually does **not** specify the exact amount or type of effect.
- To obtain **support for the scientific hypothesis**, you must collect evidence that allows you to **reject the null hypothesis**.

### 5.4 Example

- **Research question:** Does studying more improve test scores?
- **Hypothesis:** Students who study at least 8 hours a week will score higher on average on standardized tests than those who study less.
- **Independent variable:** Hours of study.
- **Dependent variable:** Test scores.

### 5.5 Importance of Testability

- In an experiment, it is the **hypotheses** derived from the research questions that are **tested**, not the research questions themselves.
- A hypothesis **must be stated** so that it is **capable of being either refuted or confirmed**.
- A hypothesis that **fails to meet the criterion of testability** removes the problem from the realm of science; any conclusion reached does not represent **scientific knowledge**.
