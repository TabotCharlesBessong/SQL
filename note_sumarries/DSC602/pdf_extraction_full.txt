01/04/26 D. L. Nkweteyim - UB-HCS606 (Human-Computer Interaction and GUI Design) 3Introduction
●What is data visualization?
–The practice of translating raw information into a visual context – such as maps or 
graphs – to make data easier for the human brain to understand and pull insights from
–Goal
○To enable our brains to identify patterns, trends, and outliers that are often 
invisible in raw numbers
●Cognitive Efficiency: Reduces the mental effort required to process large 
datasets
●Communication: Acts as a universal language for technical and non-technical 
audiences
●Decision Support: Helps stakeholders make faster, smarter, and more 
informed strategic choices.

--- PAGE 4 ---
01/04/26 D. L. Nkweteyim - UB-HCS606 (Human-Computer Interaction and GUI Design) 4Introduction
●Some definitions for data visualization
–The visual representation and presentation of data to facilitate 
understanding
–Visual representation of datasets designed to help people carry out 
tasks more e ectively ﬀ
–The use of computer-generated, interactive, visual representations of 
data to amplify cognition
–Creation of images that convey salient information about underlying 
data and Processes
–Communication of information using graphical representations

–Save time
–Have a clear purpose
–Include only the relevant content
–Encode data/information appropriately

01/04/26 D. L. Nkweteyim - UB-HCS606 (Human-Computer Interaction and GUI Design) 6Some Notes
●Visualization is suitable when there is a need to augment human 
capabilities rather than replace people with computational decision-making 
methods
●The design space of possible visualizations of data is huge
●Visualization design is full of trade-offs, and most possibilities in the design 
space are ineffective for a particular task
–Hence, need to validating the effectiveness of any design, but this is 
difﬁcult
●Visualization design should consider resource limitations viz.
–Limitations of computers, of humans, and of displays

--- PAGE 7 ---
01/04/26 D. L. Nkweteyim - UB-HCS606 (Human-Computer Interaction and GUI Design) 7Some Notes
●Why are humans involved
–Visual representation of datasets designed to help people carry out 
tasks more e ectively ﬀ
–Visualization allows people to analyze data when they don’t know 
exactly what questions they need to ask in advance
●Why external representation
–Replaces cognition with perception
○External representations (e.g., carefully designed images) augment 
human capacity by allowing us to surpass the limitations of our own 
internal cognition and memory

--- PAGE 8 ---
01/04/26 D. L. Nkweteyim - UB-HCS606 (Human-Computer Interaction and GUI Design) 8Some Notes
●Why depend on vision
–Vision is high-bandwidth interface with our brain
–Overview is possible due to background processing
○We see everything simultaneously, and the visual system processes 
in parallel and pre-attentively
–Sound has lower bandwidth
○Poorly suited for providing overviews of large information spaces 
compared with vision

--- PAGE 9 ---
01/04/26 D. L. Nkweteyim - UB-HCS606 (Human-Computer Interaction and GUI Design) 9Some Notes
●Why Show the Data in Detail?
–Visualization tools help people in situations where seeing the dataset 
structure in detail is better than seeing only a brief summary of it
–Examples
○When exploring the data to ﬁnd patterns, both to conﬁrm expected 
ones and ﬁnd unexpected ones
○When assessing the validity of a statistical model, to judge whether 
the model in fact ﬁts the data
●Statistical characterization of datasets is a great, but it has the 
intrinsic limitation of losing information through summarization 
(see example on next slide)

01/04/26 D. L. Nkweteyim - UB-HCS606 (Human-Computer Interaction and GUI Design) 11Some Notes
●Why Use Interactivity
–Crucial for building visualization tools that handle complexity
–Limitations of people and displays preclulde showing all of a large 
datasets at once
–Interactive data visualization enables users to directly manipulate 
graphical representations of data, e.g., by filtering and zooming,  to 
explore complex datasets, uncover hidden patterns, and gain deeper 
insights in real-time

●It can be hard to decide how to approach a visualization problem
●Many computer-based visualization idioms and tools have been created in the 
past several decades, and considering them one by one leaves you faced with a 
big collection of different possibilities
●Analysis framework that imposes a structure on this enormous design space and 
intended to serve as a scaffold to help you think about design choices 
systematically

–What are we visualising?
–Why are we visualising it?
–How can we visualise?

09/04/26 D. L. Nkweteyim - UB-DSC602 (Data Visualization) 5Data Types

–A discrete individual entity - such as a row in a tidy table, or a node in a 
network
●Attributes
–Also called variables or data dimensions
–Speciﬁc property that can be measured, observed, or logged

09/04/26 D. L. Nkweteyim - UB-DSC602 (Data Visualization) 6Data Types - Attributes
●Attributes types
–Categorical Data
○Does not intrinsically support arithmetic operations or a total 
ordering
○Categorical (nominal)
●No total ordering
●Examples
●Names (of brands, people, places, …)
●Movie genres
●File types

--- PAGE 7 ---
09/04/26 D. L. Nkweteyim - UB-DSC602 (Data Visualization) 7Data Types – Attribute
●Attribute types
–Ordered Data
○Data that has an implicit (total) ordering
○Two types
●Ordered (categorical)
●Also known as ordinal
●Does not support arithmetic operations
●Examples: shirt size (ordinal), rankings (ordinal)
●Quantitative
●Data that can be measured or counted
●Supports arithmetic operations
●Examples: weight, height
–

09/04/26 D. L. Nkweteyim - UB-DSC602 (Data Visualization) 8Data Types – Attribute
●Types of quantitative data
–Integers vs reals
–Interval data vs ratio data
○Interval data
●The di erence between values is meaningful ﬀ
●May not have a meaningful 0-value
○Ratio data
●The ratio between values is meaningful
●May not have a meaningful 0-value
–

--- PAGE 9 ---
09/04/26 D. L. Nkweteyim - UB-DSC602 (Data Visualization) 9Data Types – Attribute
●Interval data
–Provides ordered, ranked values with equal spacing between them, but 
the zero point is not absolute
–Can measure the distance between points, but cannot calculate ratios
○e.g., 20 °C is not "twice as hot" as 10 °C
○Examples: Temperature (Celsius/Fahrenheit), IQ scores, Calendar 
years (2024, 2025)

--- PAGE 10 ---
09/04/26 D. L. Nkweteyim - UB-DSC602 (Data Visualization) 10Data Types – Attribute
●Ratio data
–The most precise level of measurement, possessing all properties of 
interval data but with a meaningful zero point
○0 represents the total absence of the quantity
–Ratios are meaningful (e.g. 20kg  is exactly twice as heavy as 10kg
–Examples: Weight, height, time duration, distance, income.

09/04/26 D. L. Nkweteyim - UB-DSC602 (Data Visualization) 11●Ordered data – Ordering direction
–Sequential
–Diverging
–Cyclic
–

--- PAGE 12 ---
09/04/26 D. L. Nkweteyim - UB-DSC602 (Data Visualization) 12●Sequential
–Goes from a minimum value to a maximum value
–Does not have to have a meaningful basepoint or 0-value.
–Examples
○Height or weight of a person, course grades, taxation brackets

--- PAGE 13 ---
09/04/26 D. L. Nkweteyim - UB-DSC602 (Data Visualization) 13Data Types
●Diverging ordinal data
–Emerges from one neutral center in two (or, rarely, more) directions of 
progressively more extreme values.
–Examples
○Temperature (above/below freezing)
○Altitude (above/below sea level)

--- PAGE 14 ---
09/04/26 D. L. Nkweteyim - UB-DSC602 (Data Visualization) 14Data Types
● Cyclic
–An attribute is cyclic if its values wrap around to a starting point after a 
while
–Examples
○Time of day, day of week, day of year, year of century
○Also compass directions

--- PAGE 15 ---
09/04/26 D. L. Nkweteyim - UB-DSC602 (Data Visualization) 15Data Type Links
●Connections between items, usually in networks and graphs
–A link will connect two items to each other
–Examples
○Friends/follows on social networks
○Connectivity of roads or along train lines
○Family relationships
○Organizational charts

--- PAGE 16 ---
09/04/26 D. L. Nkweteyim - UB-DSC602 (Data Visualization) 16Data Type Positions
●Position data corresponds to locations in spatial data - primarily as 2-
dimensional or 3-dimensional points
●Location in space, typically given as a set of coordinates
●Examples
–Latitude and longitude
–x and y coordinates

--- PAGE 17 ---
09/04/26 D. L. Nkweteyim - UB-DSC602 (Data Visualization) 17Data Type Grids
●Grid acts as a structured framework, defined by its geometry and topology, that 
connects positions in space to data
–Examples: square grid, triangular grid, hexagonal grid
●Structure
–Grids can be uniform, regular, or irregular, and they are typically used to 
represent spatial data where each cell contains one or more data attributes
●Scalar/Vector Fields
–Grids often contain
○Scalar data (a single quantitative attribute per grid cell, like temperature) or
○Vector/tensor data (multiple attributes per cell)

--- PAGE 18 ---
09/04/26 D. L. Nkweteyim - UB-DSC602 (Data Visualization) 18Types of Datasets


--- PAGE 19 ---
09/04/26 D. L. Nkweteyim - UB-DSC602 (Data Visualization) 19Types of Datasets
●Core dataset types
–Tables
○Structured as rows (items) and columns (attributes)
○Each variable must have its own column
○Each item (observation) must have its own row
○Each value must have its own cell
○Note
●Tabular (cell) data also includes multidimensional tables (data 
hypercubes; tensors) that have composite keys

--- PAGE 20 ---
09/04/26 D. L. Nkweteyim - UB-DSC602 (Data Visualization) 20Types of Datasets
●Core dataset types
–Networks & Trees
○Represent relationships between items
○Networks consist of nodes and links
○Trees: A specific type of network without cycles, showing hierarchy
–Fields
○Represent continuous, spatial data, often defined on a grid
○They describe data that exists across a continuous space (e.g., fluid flow, 
temperature map)
–Geometry
○Data that describes the shape of objects, focusing on spatial positions

--- PAGE 21 ---
09/04/26 D. L. Nkweteyim - UB-DSC602 (Data Visualization) 21Types of Datasets
●Other Data Classifications
–Clusters, Sets, Lists
○Collections of items without the rigid structure of a table
–Static vs. Dynamic
○Static data: Entire dataset available at once
○Dynamic data: Data trickles in over time, such as streams
