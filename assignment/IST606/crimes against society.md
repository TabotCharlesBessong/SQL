# Cyber Crimes Against Society in Cameroon: Cases, Use Cases, and Impacts

## 1. Introduction

Cyber crimes against society are offences committed through digital systems that produce harm beyond one individual victim. They can destabilize trust in institutions, weaken public safety, damage economic systems, and enable exploitation at scale.

In Cameroon, these crimes now affect multiple layers of society: citizens, public institutions, businesses, vulnerable groups, and even political discourse. This report presents documented Cameroonian examples, then translates them into practical use cases for academic analysis.

## 2. What "Cyber Crimes Against Society" Means

For this assignment, "against society" includes cyber offences that:

- affect large populations or public confidence;
- undermine institutions, governance, or the justice process;
- create systemic economic loss;
- enable organized exploitation (financial, social, sexual, or political);
- cross borders and complicate law enforcement.

## 3. Cameroon Situation Snapshot (2024-2026)

Documented indicators show cybercrime pressure is increasing:

- ANTIC processed 32,500 judicial requisitions in 2025 (about 200/day), +30% from 2024.
- 8,502 vulnerabilities were identified in audits/scans of organizations.
- 8,499 fake social media accounts impersonating officials/institutions were identified since January 2024.
- 6,416 of those fake accounts were shut down through platform cooperation.

Source: Digital Business Africa reporting on ANTIC figures.

## 4. Documented Cameroon-Relevant Cases by Category

### 4.1 Online Financial Fraud and Scamming

#### Case A: SUNRUN Ponzi Scam (Cameroon)

- Modus operandi: Fraudsters presented SUNRUN as a renewable-energy investment model, falsely linked to the legitimate US company Sunrun Inc.
- Social engineering: Promise of quick returns (double/triple money), early payouts to build trust, heavy social media promotion.
- Scale: Reports estimate around 20 billion FCFA lost.
- Societal harm: Mass financial loss, erosion of trust in digital investment platforms, increased youth vulnerability to "get-rich-quick" schemes.

#### Case B: Transnational Elder Fraud Network (Cameroonian suspect)

- Pattern: Cameroonian nationals linked to money laundering for scams targeting elderly victims.
- Significance for Cameroon: Shows role of cross-border cyber-fraud ecosystems involving Cameroonian actors.

### 4.2 Identity Theft and Fake Accounts

#### Case A: Impersonation of State Officials

- 8,499 fake accounts impersonated senior officials/public institutions.
- Platforms included Facebook and TikTok.
- 6,416 accounts were removed.
- Societal impact: Fraud against citizens, disinformation spread, reduced trust in official communication channels.

#### Case B: Credential Theft / Phishing Operations (Cameroonian actor)

- Documented campaigns used spear-phishing to steal credentials and monetize stolen access.
- Societal impact: Demonstrates how identity compromise feeds broader fraud infrastructure.

### 4.3 System Hacking and Cyberattacks

#### Case A: Vehicle Sales Platform Phishing Incident (Cameroon)

- Trigger: Two victim reports.
- Response: Cameroonian investigators traced activity to a compromised server and coordinated emergency bank freezing.
- Why it matters: Illustrates operational cybercrime response capacity and importance of rapid incident escalation.

#### Case B: SIM Swap in Banking Ecosystem (Documented attack type)

- Technique: Criminals hijack victim numbers via telecom social engineering, then intercept OTPs for account access.
- Societal impact: Threatens trust in mobile money/banking channels used by a large share of the population.

### 4.4 E-Commerce Fraud

#### Case A: Fake Pet Shops Built for Fraud

- Pattern: Fake online stores advertising nonexistent animals (puppies, kittens, parrots, tortoises, etc.).
- Enabler insight: Research indicates some web developers knowingly provide technical infrastructure for scam storefronts.
- Societal impact: Normalization of fraud-as-business and reputational damage to legitimate digital commerce.

#### Case B: Social Media Fake Shops in Urban Youth Contexts

- Findings from localized studies report high participation in deceptive e-commerce practices in some communities.
- Societal impact: Weakening of trust in peer-to-peer online trade and social media marketplaces.

### 4.5 Cyber Sexual Exploitation

#### Case: Jedolo platform (cm.jedolo.com)

- Allegation: Platform linked to exploitation of underage girls.
- State response: Public condemnation and access blocking by authorities.
- Societal impact: Severe harm to minors, long-term trauma, and deep social trust and safety consequences.

### 4.6 Cyber Defamation and Digital Repression Concerns

#### Case A: Paul Chouta detention after online publication disputes

- Reported as a controversial example of cyber-related legal prosecution tied to online expression.
- Societal impact: Potential chilling effect on digital speech and civic participation.

#### Case B: Targeted account compromise allegations (political influencer context)

- Reported allegations of coordinated account intrusion.
- Societal impact: Risk to democratic discourse, political pluralism, and information integrity.

### 4.7 Online Scam Networks and Human Exploitation Risks

#### Case: INTERPOL Operations Sentinel and Red Card 2.0 (Cameroon participating)

- Red Card 2.0 outcomes across participating countries: 651 arrests, USD 4.3M recovered, 1,247 victims identified, over USD 45M linked losses.
- Sentinel outcomes: 574 arrests and approximately USD 3M recovered.
- Cameroon relevance: Participation indicates both exposure to and enforcement against transnational cybercrime ecosystems.

## 5. Labeled Use Cases (Cameroon Context)

### Use Case 1: Fake Government Account Scam

- Category: Identity theft / disinformation-enabled fraud
- Actors: Scam operator, citizen victim, social media platform, ANTIC, law enforcement
- Typical flow:
	1. Attacker clones profile/branding of ministry or senior official.
	2. Attacker publishes urgent "assistance" or "payment" instruction.
	3. Citizens transfer funds or share personal data.
	4. Platform and authorities intervene after reports.
- Social harm: Public trust erosion in government communication channels.
- Control priorities: Verified government handles, public awareness campaigns, rapid takedown protocol.

### Use Case 2: Ponzi Investment Recruitment via Social Media

- Category: Online financial fraud
- Actors: Scheme organizers, influencers/promoters, payment intermediaries, investors
- Typical flow:
	1. Platform promises abnormal profits with low risk.
	2. Early participants are paid to build social proof.
	3. Viral referrals accelerate enrollment.
	4. Operators disappear after liquidity collapse.
- Social harm: Household-level losses, debt, public distrust of digital finance.
- Control priorities: Licensing checks, payment rail monitoring, anti-fraud alerts before mass adoption.

### Use Case 3: SIM Swap to Mobile Banking Theft

- Category: System abuse / account takeover
- Actors: Fraudster, telecom insider or deceived agent, bank/mobile money platform, victim
- Typical flow:
	1. Fraudster collects victim PII (social engineering/data leakage).
	2. Number is ported/swapped to attacker SIM.
	3. OTP interception enables wallet/account reset.
	4. Rapid transfers cash out funds.
- Social harm: Direct theft, confidence loss in digital banking.
- Control priorities: SIM-swap cooling periods, telecom-bank fraud signals, stronger recovery checks.

### Use Case 4: Fake E-Commerce Storefront Fraud

- Category: E-commerce deception / non-delivery fraud
- Actors: Fraud merchant, technical enablers (site builders), victims, payment channels
- Typical flow:
	1. Fraud storefront mimics legitimate pet or retail business.
	2. Buyer pays advance fees.
	3. Seller requests extra transport/customs fees.
	4. No delivery; storefront disappears or rebrands.
- Social harm: Consumer confidence decline and reputational harm to local digital entrepreneurs.
- Control priorities: Marketplace verification, escrow mechanisms, domain-intelligence blacklists.

### Use Case 5: Platform-Enabled Sexual Exploitation of Minors

- Category: Cyber sexual exploitation
- Actors: Exploiters, minors, platform operators, state agencies, civil society
- Typical flow:
	1. Recruitment/coercion through digital channels.
	2. Distribution/monetization via web platform.
	3. Delayed reporting due to stigma/fear.
	4. Takedown and legal response after evidence consolidation.
- Social harm: Severe individual and community trauma.
- Control priorities: Child-protection cyber units, evidence preservation workflows, victim-support pipelines.

## 6. Cross-Case Pattern Analysis

Across these categories, repeated patterns appear:

- Social engineering is central: trust is manipulated more often than systems are deeply hacked.
- Platform abuse is scalable: fake accounts/pages can reach thousands quickly.
- Cybercrime is hybrid: online deception often combines with offline logistics, cashout networks, and local recruiters.
- Youth vulnerability is high: unemployment, instability, and digital aspiration increase exposure (as victims and, at times, enablers).
- Response capacity is improving: takedowns, forensic processing, and cross-border operations are more visible, but attack volume remains high.

## 7. Policy and Security Recommendations (Report-Ready)

- Institutional verification: Mandatory verified channels for all ministries and high-profile public services.
- National anti-scam watchlist: Public registry of flagged investment apps/domains/pages.
- Telecom-bank fusion center: Shared real-time fraud indicators for SIM swap and account takeover detection.
- Digital evidence acceleration: Expand forensic lab capacity and standardized chain-of-custody protocols.
- Youth-focused prevention: Campus and community cyber-literacy modules focused on scam recruitment patterns.
- Platform accountability: Faster legal pathways for emergency takedown of impersonation and child exploitation content.
